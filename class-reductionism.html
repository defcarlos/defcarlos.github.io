Who’s afraid of class reductionism?

Where I ask you to consider the salve of conferalism.

When the Democratic Socialist of America (DSA) canceled a talk they scheduled with Adolph Reed back in 2020, I became aware of the term class reductionism and I’m sure many others did too. At its simplest, the term means that a person explains all social ills through class and all other concerns and struggles - sexism, racism, LGBTQ+, etc. should be ignored because they will be improved once class issues are addressed. Implisit in the term is the suggestion that a class reductionist is bigoted or doesn’t care about the struggles faced by particular identity groups.

Adolph Reed and DSA affiliated magazine, Jacobin, have argued that there is no such thing as class reductionism. Like with many such arguments, people on either side are firmly entrenched in their views and aren’t likely to change their minds. I am sympathetic to Adolph Reed and others of his ilk. As far as I understand their argument, they are trying to address the disunity they observe on the left and rally around a theme that cuts across all identities. When the key organizational component is identity and concerns specific to that identity, it’s hard to create the mass movement they believe is required for substantive change. They point to many of the rights won during the early part of the 20th century through organizing the working class. I can also understand the identitarian side of things. To engage in class struggle and secure the rights pointed to these identitarian groups’ concerns have had to take a backseat.

Tensions are made worse when such a heterogeneous group hangs all of its specific hopes and dreams on one individual (a leader or figurehead). This could be an individual who is supposed to work within the system but somehow not be a part of the system. No matter how incredibly progressive and thoughtful a person might be, they cannot keep all of the demands of such a patchwork of groups in mind when trying to push forward a campaign or policy. A group will inevitably feel slighted or ignored. This is essentially the tension present during Bernie Sander’s catastrophic presidential run in 2016.

This tension between class and identity is not unique to Marxism or Democratic Socialism. Indeed we can see it appear in the variety of Anarchist groups with adjectives. I suspect it’s a tension that has existed since the dawn of politics.

What is to be done? I think something like communalism/confederalism/municipalism have the raw materials we can borrow or emulate so that we can come closer to meeting everyone’s needs. The municipalities are autonomous or semi-autnomous and make decisions that impact their locality. Additional, steps can be taken to help mitigate leaving a group out of the discussion. For example, the Democratic Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria (commonly known in the West as Rojava) has a man and a woman as equal delegates or representatives for their village, region, etc. They do not make decisions for their municipality or village when there is larger regional discussion but, instead express the interest of their group and bring back information for further decisions to be made. While this might sound potentially inefficient, I would like to point out the only time the US government is ever efficient in their decision making is because there’s money to be made by themselves or their capitalist overlords.

There’s a whole body of literature related to the communalism/confederalism/municipalism impulse. I don’t want to recreate the wheel. I hope you can see the possibilities for something inclusive, egalitarian, and possibly sustainable as long as the citizens of the confederation avoid the want for kings.
